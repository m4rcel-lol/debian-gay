Why Debian Is GAY

Not literally. Just in the sense of being annoying, old, stubborn, and weirdly proud of making normal desktop users do extra homework.

See the 10 reasons Better distros instead

Debian summary

Incredible as a base. Respectable on servers. On the desktop? Often feels like pain dressed up as discipline.

  • Old packages
  • Extra setup
  • Desktop boredom
  • Better options exist

What this page means

This page is a joke roast, but the complaints are real. Debian is not unusable. It is just a rough daily-driver choice for a lot of normal desktop users who want modern hardware support, current apps, easier gaming, and less ritual suffering.

Debian fans will call that “stability.” Everyone else calls it “why is my laptop newer than the entire repo.”

10 good reasons Debian can suck as a daily desktop

Sharp, sarcastic, and mostly deserved.

01

Packages are too old on Stable

Debian Stable worships reliability so hard that apps, drivers, and dev tools can feel ancient. Great if you run a museum. Less great if you use a computer from this decade.

02

Bad for very new hardware

Fresh laptop, fresh GPU, fresh Wi-Fi card? Nice. Now enjoy discovering that your distro thinks modern hardware is a rumor.

03

Conservative desktop experience

Debian desktop often feels like an OS designed by people who consider fun to be suspicious. Safe, sure. Exciting? Absolutely not.

04

More manual setup for codecs and proprietary stuff

Media playback, firmware, little desktop conveniences: Debian loves making those feel like side quests instead of defaults.

05

NVIDIA setup can be annoying

Yes, you can make it work. That is not the same as it being pleasant. Debian desktop has a special talent for turning basic convenience into lore.

06

Worse for gaming

Older kernel, older Mesa, older graphics stack. Meanwhile other distros are already playing the game while Debian is still reading the forum thread.

07

Testing and Unstable defeat the point

If you leave Stable to get newer software, you also leave the whole reason Debian people keep defending Debian. At that point, why not just use something better for desktop life?

08

Slow release cadence

Fantastic for servers. Less fantastic for humans who want newer software before retirement age.

09

Weak beginner friendliness compared to alternatives

Debian is not impossible, but Mint and Ubuntu are just easier. Debian often feels like the OS equivalent of being assigned extra work because you finished your main task too early.

10

Better as a base than as a finished desktop distro

Debian is amazing underneath many things. That does not mean raw Debian is the thing most people should actually use every day on their main desktop.

Better daily distros instead

Pick the pain level you actually want.

Fedora Workstation

Best balanced daily driver

Modern packages, polished desktop, strong hardware support, less fossil energy than Debian.

Linux Mint

Best easy desktop

Friendly, stable, comfy, and low-drama. For people who want to use the computer, not date it.

Ubuntu LTS

Stable but desktop-friendly

Still dependable, but far less raw and joyless than plain Debian for typical desktop use.

Arch Linux

Latest everything

For advanced users who want control, current packages, and the occasional spiritual test.

EndeavourOS

Arch, less ceremonial

Modern, flexible, much less annoying than doing Arch fully raw from scratch.

CachyOS

Fast Arch-like pick

Fresh packages, desktop focus, gaming appeal, and fewer “enjoy configuring your afternoon away” moments.

Lubuntu

Great for old hardware

Practical, light, and much smarter than forcing a heavier Debian desktop onto weak machines.

MX Linux

Light but comfortable

Excellent for weaker systems and people who want sensible tools instead of pure distro austerity.

Recommendation summary

  • Normal daily use / school / coding / browsing → Fedora
  • Easiest desktop → Linux Mint
  • Stable but user-friendly → Ubuntu LTS
  • Latest everything → Arch / EndeavourOS / CachyOS
  • Old hardware → Lubuntu / MX Linux

Debian is excellent if your dream operating system gives you old packages, mild inconvenience, and the chance to pretend pain is a personality trait.

For servers: respectable. For daily desktop life: install something that actually likes you back.
